# Project: 1-on-1 Automation System at Sora Inc.
- Project type: individual internship project, internal business improvement, automation system, requirement definition, AWS automation, Notion and Slack integration.
- Stakeholders: internal promotion team, interns, mentors, and Yu as the developer.
- Problem: the internal team manually checked Notion data, confirmed monthly 1-on-1 pairs, and posted results to Slack every month.
- Business pain: the work took about 3 hours per month, became repetitive, depended on human operation, and could grow as the number of interns increased.
- Solution: build a system that reads Notion databases, checks target users, posts Slack messages, and prepares the next monthly matching table.
- Core technologies: Python, AWS Lambda, EventBridge Scheduler, Notion API, Slack API, AWS SAM, GitHub Actions, Docker, Dev Container, SSM Parameter Store, and CloudWatch Logs.
- Operational schedule: on the 22nd of each month, the system checks matching status and reports issues; on the 24th, it posts current matching results and prepares the next matching table.
- Reason for two Lambda functions: the 22nd report should not update the Matching database, while the 24th function posts and shuffles data; separating them improves safety, clarity, and maintainability.
- Lambda 1: 1on1-report-automation checks errors and warnings and posts a report to a report channel.
- Lambda 2: 1on1-slack-automation posts the official 1-on-1 matching result and creates the next matching table.
- Notion databases: InternManagement, MentorManagement, and Matching.
- InternManagement fields: internName, slackIntern, department or departmentIntern, and internStatus.
- MentorManagement fields: mentorName, slackMentor, department or departmentMentor, and mentorStatus.
- Matching fields: matchingID, mentorName, and internName, with a mentor-to-intern relationship.
- Slack mention rule: Slack mentions should use member IDs stored in slackIntern and slackMentor, such as <@Uxxxx>.
- Preferred Slack output: one parent message per Matching row, not thread-based messages.
- Status design: status was simplified to target and non-target to reduce operational confusion.
- Matching rules: use only target mentors and target interns, avoid pairing people from the same department, avoid repeating previous mentor-intern pairs, do not revive non-target users automatically, and reduce imbalance among mentors when possible.
- Error design: if no valid matching is possible, the system should make it clear which condition may be too strict.
- Important design decision: people intentionally removed from Matching should not always be treated as system errors; some cases should become warnings or reports.
- Communication challenge: Yu had to explain Lambda, APIs, scheduling, and database constraints to non-engineers without technical jargon.
- Communication solution: he described the system in business language, such as “on the 22nd, the system checks Notion and reports missing target users to Slack.”
- Automation philosophy: not everything should be automated blindly; the system should support human confirmation where business exceptions exist.
- Future improvements: manage code in GitHub, unify the development environment with Docker and Dev Containers, build CI/CD with GitHub Actions, manage AWS resources with SAM, store secrets in SSM Parameter Store, monitor errors with CloudWatch Logs, and add pytest for status, department, and shuffle logic.
- Resume version: designed and developed an automated 1-on-1 matching system using Notion API, AWS Lambda, EventBridge, and Slack API, reducing monthly manual work by about 89%.
- Interview version: this project shows Yu’s ability to identify operational bottlenecks, define requirements with non-engineers, design a practical system, and deliver measurable business impact.

# Case Answer Framework
- Step 1: do not answer immediately; first organize the situation.
- Step 2: set evaluation criteria such as urgency, impact, dependency, user impact, operational risk, cost, and feasibility.
- Step 3: split items into groups instead of treating everything equally.
- Step 4: move with a hypothesis when information is incomplete, but define what must be checked.
- Step 5: communicate the decision and reasoning to stakeholders.
- Step 6: review the result and improve the process.

# Case Answer: 100 Tasks
- I would not handle 100 tasks as equal. First, I would classify them by urgency, impact, dependency, and risk. I would identify fixed-deadline tasks, tasks blocking other people, tasks with direct user or business impact, and tasks that can be postponed or removed. Then I would create today, this week, later, and not-do categories. My priority is not to work hard on everything, but to focus on the most important result.

# Case Answer: Everything Is Important
- If everything is called important, I would first clarify what “important” means. Is it revenue impact, user satisfaction, risk reduction, deadline, or stakeholder expectation? After aligning the evaluation criteria, I would compare tasks again. In practice, everything cannot have exactly the same priority, so defining the meaning of importance is necessary before execution.

# Case Answer: Deadline Tomorrow and Five Tasks Remain
- I would not try to complete all five at the same quality level. First, I would define the minimum acceptable quality for each task, separate must-have from nice-to-have, and check dependencies and external impact. If risk remains, I would communicate early with the current status, reason, alternative plan, and what can be delivered by the deadline.

# Case Answer: Ambiguous Requirements
- I would not start building with vague requirements. I would divide them into must-have, nice-to-have, and needs-confirmation. Then I would ask concrete example-based questions, such as who uses it, when they use it, what happens in exception cases, and what must not happen. I believe requirement definition is not about pretending everything is clear; it is about correctly separating uncertainty.

# Case Answer: Unrealistic Non-Engineer Request
- I would not simply say “impossible.” First, I would ask what goal they are trying to achieve. Then I would offer options: what can be done now, what can be done with changed conditions, and what is difficult because of technical or operational constraints. I would explain through business impact rather than technical jargon.

# Case Answer: App Improvement
- I would choose an app where users face friction before reaching value, such as too many setup steps or unclear next actions. My improvement would be to reduce initial input, show only the next necessary step, and add feedback after the first small success. I would validate this through onboarding completion rate, time-to-first-value, retention, and user comments.

# Case Answer: Speed vs Performance
- I would decide based on user value and product phase. If performance issues are already causing user drop-off or operational cost, performance improvement becomes urgent. If the product is not validated yet, a new feature or MVP may matter more. I do not optimize for beauty alone; I optimize for delivered value, risk, and learning speed.

# Case Answer: Production Incident
- First, I would check impact and stop damage before deep root-cause analysis. I would identify who is affected, whether rollback is possible, and what temporary mitigation can reduce impact. Then I would communicate status, isolate the cause, restore service, and document prevention steps. In an incident, transparency and structured response matter more than hiding the mistake.

# Case Answer: User Complaints but Metrics Look Fine
- I would not ignore either side. I would check whether the complaints come from a specific user segment, device, flow, or edge case that aggregate metrics hide. Quantitative data and qualitative feedback should be used together. The goal is to align the resolution of the data with the actual user experience.
